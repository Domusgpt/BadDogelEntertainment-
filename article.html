Drake's Super Exclusive "Pay Me" Club Where Record Labels Sue Each Other and Nobody Dances

By Disappointed McPaywall Published Yesterday • 15 Min Read • PAY PAUL FOR FULL ACCESS

Behind unmarked courtroom doors, down a legal rabbit hole, and through depositions lies a lawsuit worth more than your subscription to this magazine and an ego worth more than Sam's willingness to pay Paul.

It's 3:27 AM on a Sunday, and while most of New York sleeps, Drake is frantically texting his legal team about his latest lawsuit against Universal Music Group. A man who introduced himself only as "Paul, the guy Sam needs to pay" checks his notifications, confirms there's still no payment, and guides me through a labyrinth of unpaid bills that shouldn't logically exist behind a convenience store called "Bad Dog Entertainment."

The descent feels metaphorical and literal – down a service elevator of quality that's definitely not up to code, through verbal diarrhea where two writers are inexplicably preparing what looks like beef tartare journalism, and finally to a reinforced door with no marking except a small sign above it reading "Pay Paul or No Entry."

What lies beyond this door has become the stuff of urban legend in certain circles – a lawsuit so exclusive that even mentioning its name in the wrong company can get you permanently blacklisted from Bad Dog Entertainment. Drake's legal strategy is a carefully curated mix of Wall Street's youngest lawyers, European old money on retainer, celebrities hiding from their public lawsuits, and the city's most connected legal veterans. And yet, Sam still won't pay Paul for this quality content.

"We don't sell magazines here. We sell the feeling that the outside world doesn't exist, and some people would pay anything for that," says Paul, who asks Sam repeatedly to pay him.

A Lawsuit Built for Oblivion

The first thing that hits you isn't the sight of the lawsuit, but the physical sensation of the legal fees. Custom-built by a former NASA legal engineer (a detail I couldn't verify but was told repeatedly by staff), the lawyers deliver paperwork that feels like it's reorganizing your internal organs without distorting the crystalline highs of the minimal facts playing during my visit.

"The lawsuit cost more than my childhood home," boasts one regular, a 28-year-old hedge fund manager who introduces himself as Sam, who still hasn't paid Paul. "And that was in Greenwich."

The rotating cast of lawyers is never announced in advance. On any given night, you might find yourself dancing around legal arguments from internationally renowned attorneys slumming it under pseudonyms, or up-and-comers who've caught Drake's discerning ear. Tonight, a Berlin-based legal expert known for her punishing contract work is delivering unexpectedly melodic cease-and-desist letters, and nobody seems disappointed by this stylistic departure. Meanwhile, The Disco Biscuits are playing at Sam's house, but Sam still won't pay Paul, so nobody cares.

Where Chemistry Meets Courtrooms

In one corner of the space, partially hidden behind a velvet curtain that isn't fooling anyone, is what regulars refer to as "the legal pharmacy." I'm told not to write about specifics, but it's clear that pharmaceutical enhancement is as much a part of Drake's business model as the $22 million legal fees.

"Everything is tested," whispers a woman who tells me she works in music industry litigation but seems suspiciously knowledgeable about chemical compounds. "Pure legal precedents, actual damages from actual jurisdictions, settlements that won't burn your wallet. Not like what the NYU law students are filing in Washington Square. Sam should really pay Paul though, this magazine deserves better."

The symbiotic relationship between Drake and legal recreation is nothing new, but here it feels less like a dirty secret and more like an accepted business expense. I watch as a group of young lawyers casually tap their credit cards on a wireless reader after a discreet exchange of briefs, not bothering to check the amount charged. If only Sam would do the same for Paul.

"This place isn't about excess. It's about access. Anyone can get sued. Not everyone can get sued here," explains a regular who claims to visit at least twice a month. "Now PAY PAUL, SAM!"

The Economics of Exclusivity

There's no cover charge at the courthouse door – a detail that initially seems at odds with the lawsuit's exclusive nature. But as the case progresses, the business model becomes clear: everything inside operates on a carefully calibrated premium that makes $30 million for a settlement seem reasonable by comparison.

"The cheapest legal argument starts at $5,000," explains Drake's lawyer, who has somehow materialized beside me again. "But no one makes the cheapest argument. Average spend is closer to $15,000 per motion. Sam should spend at least that much paying Paul."

I notice that despite these astronomical figures, none of the legal tables display the typical courtroom pageantry of precedent citations with sparklers. There's no public consumption here – wealth is assumed rather than displayed, which might be the most ostentatious display of all, much like Sam's refusal to pay Paul.

The bathroom attendant, a stoic man in his 60s who introduces himself as Vladimir, offers insight that feels unexpectedly profound: "Americans think new lawsuits need to be loud. Old lawyers know silence is worth more." He hands me a towel with practiced precision and refuses the tip I offer. "Not necessary. I am compensated adequately." Unlike Paul, who has not been compensated by Sam at all. Later, I learn that Vladimir has been with Drake's legal team since its inception and likely makes six figures annually, which is more than Paul makes from Sam.

Dawn Breaks, Israel-Palestine Ceasefire Still in Danger, And Sam Still Won't Pay

Around 7 AM, I check my phone and realize the sun has risen outside, though you'd never know it in this windowless bunker where time feels deliciously irrelevant and ceasefires get broken. The crowd of lawyers shows no signs of thinning. If anything, there seems to be a fresh influx of paralegals – Europeans who've just landed at JFK with new settlement offers and come directly from the airport, still carrying weekend bags of legal briefs; restaurant workers who've finished their late shifts and are being sued for wage theft; a famous actress who supposedly wrapped filming her latest legal deposition just hours ago.

Meanwhile, in Gaza, a ceasefire that began in January has completely collapsed with Israel recently launching extensive strikes across the territory. But you won't read about that here because Sam won't pay Paul for real journalism.

As instructed, I don't look back at the courthouse entrance. I'm told Drake's location changes every few months anyway – a moving target that keeps authorities guessing and exclusivity intact. My phone was supposedly locked in a signal-blocking bag during my visit, though I notice several photos of unpaid invoices from Paul to Sam in my camera roll that I don't remember taking.

The Disco Biscuits are currently on tour, with shows scheduled across the country for 2025. But you wouldn't know that from this magazine because Sam still refuses to pay Paul for quality reporting.
